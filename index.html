Universal Design

Making intention decisions to increase participation and engagement in exhibits.

This presentation translates the universal design review into practical takeaways for Children’s Museum of Oak Lawn staff. It focuses on how exhibit environments support or limit participation, where barriers emerged, and what realistic actions can strengthen play opportunities for a broader range of visitors.

What staff should take away

Universal design is not about becoming designers. It is about noticing how the environment helps children get into play or how it can unintentionally create extra work for them and their caregivers.

Main pattern found

Many spaces supported flexible play, but barriers still appeared in access, clarity of use, communication supports, and physical effort.

Practical goal

Preserve open-ended play while improving entry points, access, and usability for a wider range of visitors.

What is universal design?

Universal design means creating spaces that can be used by as many people as possible without needing special adaptation.

✓
Can children figure out how to begin?
Entry into play should be understandable without relying only on adult explanation.
✓
Can they reach and use materials?
Physical access, storage, positioning, and effort all shape who can participate.
✓
Are there multiple ways to engage?
Flexible environments support different play styles, body sizes, ages, and abilities.
✓
Does the space support independence as well as shared play?
Caregiver involvement can be valuable, but barriers should not make it required.

What UD is not

  • Not just ADA compliance
    It goes beyond minimum accessibility requirements.
  • Not removing challenge
    Children’s play can still be active, effortful, and exploratory.
  • Not one “right” way to play
    UD supports optional entry points, not rigid rules.
  • Not only for one group
    It benefits many users by making participation easier to understand and access.

How this project was done

The review was developed to be meaningful for museum use: grounded in universal design principles, informed by needs assessment findings, and tested in the live museum environment.

Step 1

Needs assessment

Visitor and staff input, along with observations, helped identify priority exhibits and recurring participation concerns.

Step 2

Tool development

A structured review tool was created using the seven universal design principles and museum/accessibility guidance.

Step 3

Exhibit review

The tool was used to observe selected exhibits during museum hours to identify strengths, barriers, and opportunities.

Why these four exhibits?

Exhibit selection was tied to needs assessment findings so the review would focus on spaces that were already meaningful to museum stakeholders.

Build It

Selected because it emerged as an exhibit that could use improvement and/or was underutilized.

Gear Wall

Also identified as an exhibit that could use improvement and/or was underutilized.

Tree House

Selected because it is a highly popular exhibit and important to children’s museum play.

What If

Selected based on structured staff interview input.

Why this matters

This combination allowed the review to include both high-use spaces and areas where staff saw room for change.

What the review tool looks for

The tool is descriptive, not just a scorecard. It helps staff notice how exhibit design supports or limits participation.

Equitable Use

Can a range of children access the core play activity?

Flexibility in Use

Can children engage in more than one way?

Simple & Intuitive Use

Is it clear how to begin and continue play?

Perceptible Information

Is information communicated in more than one way?

Tolerance for Error

Can children safely try, experiment, and keep going?

Low Physical Effort

Are demands reasonable or adjustable?

Size & Space

Is there enough room to approach and use the exhibit?

Feasible actions museum staff can take

These are practical steps staff can use to improve access and participation without changing the open-ended nature of play.

Add visual entry points

Use universal symbols, simple pictures, or leaving one example setup to show children how they might begin interacting with an exhibit. For example, leaving an example set up in Build It with materials.

Rethink material placement

Move frequently used materials into more visible and reachable locations, and consider using open or angled storage instead of low closed bins when possible.

Reduce text-only instructions

Pair written directions with icons, photos, color-coding, or visual prompts so more visitors can understand how to engage.

Watch for caregiver reliance

If children consistently need adults to reach, carry, or explain materials, that may point to a barrier in the environment rather than the child. This can help distinguish between a child needing support and an environment that could be adjusted.

Notice congestion and access

Pay attention to where exhibits become crowded, blocked, or harder to approach during busy periods, and adjust layout or materials when realistic.

Recommendations by level of change

These recommendations are organized from shorter-term, lower-resource changes to longer-term planning considerations.

Children frequently relied on caregivers or peers to understand how to start. Staff can reduce that barrier by using pictures, symbols, pre-assembled examples of exhibit materials (examples Gear Wall, Build It), or prompts like “Try this” or “What can you do?” rather than step-by-step directions.
In Build It, storage location for some materials were less visible and less likely to draw attention. In Gear Wall, storage location may limit access for some users. Adjustments to storage height, open display, lighting, and positioning can support more independent use.
When exhibit information is communicated mostly through text, some visitors may miss or not use it. Icons, color-coding, contrast, tactile markers, and short visual examples can make engagement more accessible without over-directing play.
The biggest long-term value of this project is not just the findings from four exhibits. It is having a practical framework staff can use when updating current exhibits, planning new features, or discussing visitor usability concerns.
The report identifies future opportunities such as braille paired with clear contrast, audio-based supports, and sensory or social stories that help visitors prepare for the museum experience. These supports are optional layers, not replacements for open-ended play.

Why this matters for staff and the museum

This review is useful because it creates a shared operational language around participation, not just design theory.

Shared language

Gives staff a clearer way to talk about exhibit usability, access, and participation.

Earlier problem-solving

Helps identify barriers before they become larger visitor experience issues.

Better decisions

Supports more intentional choices about exhibit updates, layouts, and communication supports.

Future planning

The tool can be reused for future review, reflection, and planning conversations.

Visitor experience

Small changes can make play easier to enter, easier to sustain, and more independent for more families.

Universal design is not about making every exhibit the same. It is about making sure more children have meaningful ways to enter, engage in, and sustain play. Small, intentional changes can significantly expand how children access, engage in, and sustain play across the museum.
